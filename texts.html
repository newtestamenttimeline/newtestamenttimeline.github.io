New Testament Texts - Provenance, Dating, and Evidence

Introduction to the New Testament Texts

The New Testament consists of 27 books, written by various authors across different regions, addressing early Christian communities and doctrines. These texts are central to Christianity and have been preserved in numerous manuscripts over centuries. The following sections explore the provenance, likely date of writing, manuscript evidence, historical evidence, and extrabiblical citations of the key New Testament texts.

The Gospels

The four canonical Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — are the cornerstone of the New Testament, recounting the life, ministry, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. They are traditionally attributed to the apostles or their close associates and were written between 65 AD and 100 AD.

Provenance and Likely Date of Writing

  • Gospel of Mark: Likely written in Rome around 65-70 AD, the Gospel of Mark is generally considered the earliest of the four Gospels. Scholars believe it served as a source for Matthew and Luke.
  • Gospel of Matthew: Written around 80-90 AD, likely in Antioch. This Gospel incorporates Jewish traditions and teachings, presenting Jesus as the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy.
  • Gospel of Luke: Likely written between 85-95 AD, possibly in Greece or Antioch, and intended for a Gentile audience. It is part of a two-volume work that includes Acts.
  • Gospel of John: The latest of the four Gospels, written around 90-100 AD, likely in Ephesus. It offers a theological perspective on Jesus' life and divinity.
