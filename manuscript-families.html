Manuscript Families

Manuscript families, or "text-types," are groups of manuscripts that share common textual characteristics and geographical origins. By comparing manuscripts from the same family, scholars can trace the history of textual variations and understand how the New Testament was transmitted in different regions of the early Christian world.

Alexandrian Family

The Alexandrian family of manuscripts is one of the earliest and most reliable sources of the New Testament. These manuscripts are often associated with Alexandria, Egypt, a major center of early Christian scholarship.

Byzantine Family

The Byzantine family represents the majority of surviving New Testament manuscripts. Originating in the Byzantine Empire, these texts became the standard version of the New Testament in Greek-speaking churches.

Western Family

The Western family of manuscripts, associated with the Western Roman Empire, often exhibits paraphrasing and additional textual variation compared to the Alexandrian and Byzantine families.

Caesarean Family

This family of manuscripts is believed to have originated in Caesarea in the 3rd or 4th century. It is known for blending features of both the Alexandrian and Western text-types.
